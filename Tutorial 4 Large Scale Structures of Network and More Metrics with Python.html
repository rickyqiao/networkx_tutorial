1.1 Power law¶

Degree distribution $P(k)$ is often an interesting quantity in a large network. If we plot it against degree $k$, we often see a long tail, which signifies some power law. Indeed, we can see a negatively linear trend if we make a log-log plot of $P(k)$ against $k$.

\begin{align} \ln\left.P(k)\right. &\sim -a\ln\left.k\right. \left(a>0\right) \\ \Rightarrow P(k) &\sim k^{-a} \end{align}
In [4]:

Is it trivial for a degree distribution to follow some power law? If we measure the degree distribution of all networks with $N$ nodes and $E$ edges, do we expect to observe a power-law frequently? We may answer this question by inspecting the average degree distribution $\overline{P}_\mathcal{R}{(k)}$ of a random network $\mathcal{R}$.

You can construct a random network with gnm_random_graph(n, m), which draws $m$ out of all $\left(\matrix{n\\2}\right)$ possible edges on an $n$-node edgeless graph.

1.2 Visualization¶

Although it is the most faithful to plot $P(k)$ at all available $k$, we usually bin our data before plotting. This has several advantages.

  1. Binning smooths out the distribution.
  2. Binning reduces the chance of having zeros, i.e. $P(k)=0$, which interrupt our log-log plot.
  3. Binning tidies up the data, especially when $k$ spans a wide range.

However, there is no optimal rule to determine how to bin the data, so binning introduces inevitably much bias to our analysis. Check Histogram on Wikipedia for more information.

In [6]:

    log = kwargs.pop('log', False)

        centers = (binEdges[1:]*binEdges[:-1])**.5
    else:
        centers = (binEdges[1:]+binEdges[:-1])*.5
    ax.plot(centers, probs, fmt, **kwargs)

In fact, one may consider plotting the distribution's complimentary CDF (cCDF) $P(X>k)$ to avoid binning, where $X$ denotes an arbitrary node's degree. If $P(k)\sim k^{-a}$, the corresponding cCDF also obeys some power law:

\begin{align} P(X>k) \sim \int_k^\infty x^{-a} d{x} \sim \left(x^{-a+1}\right)^\infty_k \sim k^{-a+1} \,. \end{align}

While $P(X>k)$ behaves like $P(k)$ in this aspect, it is no way rugged but monotocally decreasing, so it is automatically smooth without binning; furthermore, it definitely has no zeros at $k\in[k_{\min}, k_{\max})$, so we can plot its log-log plot safely.

1.2 Degree exponent¶

For $P(k)\sim k^{-a}$, we refer to $a$ as the degree exponent. Network scientists are interested in measuring a degree distribution's degree exponent. A simple yet poor approach applies the method of least squares and fits $\ln{P(X>k)}$ with $\left[{\left({-a+1}\right)\ln{k}+b}\right]$.

\begin{align} \ln{P(X>k)} &= \left({-a+1}\right)\ln{k}+b \\ \Rightarrow P(X>k) &= e^b k^{-a+1} \end{align}

You need more statistical techinques, e.g. regularized regression, hypothesis testing, and maximum likelihood estimation, to obtain a more reliable $a$.

2. More on Power Law¶

"Power law" is just a generic term that describes any distribution that obeys $P(x)\sim x^{-a}$, but what does that little "$\sim$" mean? To make the relationship rigorous, we must specify some normalization constant $A$ in $P(x)=Ax^{-a}$ so that $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}{P(x)dx}=1$. In fact, a number of distributions satisfy the canonical formula but differ from each other with $A$ due to their different supports of $x$, i.e. the domain of $x$ over which $P(x)>0$.

2.1 Pareto distribution¶

If $x\in\mathbb{R}$ and supports $[m,\infty)$, a power law corresponds to the Pareto distribution $f_P{(x; a, m)}$.

\begin{align} A_P = \left( \int_m^\infty {x^{-a}dx} \right)^{-1} = \left[ \left(\dfrac{x^{-a+1}}{-a+1}\right)^\infty_{m} \right]^{-1} = \dfrac{a-1}{m^{-a+1}} \end{align}

Conventionally, the Pareto distribution is defined after shifting $a$ by $+1$, so

\begin{align} f_P{(x; a, m)} = \dfrac{(a+1)-1}{m^{-(a+1)+1}} x^{-(a+1)} = \dfrac{am^a}{x^{a+1}}\,. \end{align}

You can learn more about it with stats.pareto from SciPy, which sets $m=1$ by default.

In [10]:

2.2 Zipf distribution¶

If $x\in\mathbb{N}$ and supports $[m,M]$, a power law refers to the Zipf distribution $f_Z{(x;a,m,M)}$.

\begin{align} f_Z{(x;a,m,M)} = \dfrac{x^{-a}}{\sum_{\xi=m}^M \xi^{-a}} \end{align}

The sum in its denominator shrinks to the $a$-th order harmonic number of $M$, i.e. $H_{M,a}$, for $m=1$. Some only refer to the Zipf distribution as the distribution with $m=1$ and call the one with $m\geq1$ the Zipf-Mandelbrot distribution instead.

2.3 Zeta distribution¶

If $x\in\mathbb{N}$ and supports $[m,\infty)$, a power law refers to the zeta distribution $f_\zeta{(x;a,m)}$.

\begin{align} f_\zeta{(x;a,m)} = \dfrac{x^{-a}}{\zeta(a,m)} \equiv \dfrac{x^{-a}}{\sum_{\xi=0}^\infty (\xi+m)^{-a}} \end{align}

$\zeta(a,m)$ is called the Hurwitz zeta function, which generalizes the Riemann zeta function. Note that the zeta funciton and thus the distribution diverge at $a=1$.

You can learn more about it with stats.**zipf** (not anything like .*zeta*) from SciPy, which sets $m=1$ by default.

2.4 Exponential distribution¶

Although the lin-lin plot of the exponential distribution $f_e{(x;a,m)}$ looks like that of the Pareto distribution, it does not belong to the family of power law; its tail decays much faster than one that obeys a power law.

\begin{align} f_e{(x;a,m)} &= \begin{cases} ae^{-a(x-m)} & (x\geq m) \\ 0 & (x<m) \end{cases} \\ \Rightarrow \ln f_e{(x;a,m)} &= \begin{cases} -a(x-m)+\ln{a} & (x\geq m) \\ \text{undefined} & (x<m) \end{cases} \end{align}

As $\ln{f_e}\sim x$, one may reveal an exponential distribution's identity by plotting it on a log-lin plot.

We would like to quantify the extent of a network's clustering, i.e. how much a node's neighbourhood correlate with its neighbours' ones. A network without any clusters is simply a tree, while trees can hardly model real networks (, otherwise your friends are never friends).

3.1 Clustering coefficient¶

A node's clustering coefficient measures how probably its neighbours are connected and thus form a triangle. For a $k$-degree node with $e$ edges among its neighbours, its clustering coefficient reads

\begin{align} c = \dfrac{2e}{k(k-1)}\,. \end{align}

As there are at most $\dfrac{k(k-1)}{2}$ edges among $k$ nodes, $c$ definitely lie between $0$ and $1$. The higher a node's clustering coefficient is, the more probably its neighbours are connected. You can compute a node's clustering coefficient with clustering, which returns a dictionary.

3.2 Transitivity¶

A network's transitivity $C_\Delta$ measures how probably three nodes form a triangle if they already form a triad, i.e. a V-shaped structure. The qualitative definition of $C_\Delta$ reads

\begin{align} C_\Delta = 3\times\dfrac{\text{#triangles}} {\text{#triads}}\,, \end{align}

where the leading factor compensates the fact that each triangle comprises three triads. One may refine the definition with the network's adjacency matrix $\mathbf{A}$:

\begin{align} C_\Delta = \dfrac{\text{tr}\left(\mathbf{A}^3\right)} {\sum_{i\neq j}\left(\mathbf{A}^2\right)_{ij}}\,. \end{align}

Try to reason why the two defintions are equivalent. After all, you can compute the quantity with transitivity.

While some refer to a network's clustering coefficient $C$ as its transitivity $C_\Delta$, some actually refer to $C$ as the network's average clustering coefficeint $\langle{c}\rangle$, which you can compute with average_clustering.

In [18]:
nx.transitivity(copperfield), nx.average_clustering(copperfield)
Out[18]:
(0.15693497881746177, 0.1728400798103679)

The two quantities generally differ although they both count triangles. This occurs due to their formal difference: $C_\Delta$ is a ratio of sums, whereas $\langle{c}\rangle$ amounts to an average of fractions.

\begin{align} C_\Delta &\sim \dfrac{\sum_i \text{# triangles involving $i$}} {\sum_i \text{# triads involving $i$}} \\ \\ \langle{c}\rangle &\sim \sum_i \dfrac{\text{# triangles involving $i$}} {\text{# triads involving $i$}} \end{align}

3.3 Reciprocity¶

On directed networks, one may measure reciprocity $R$, i.e. the probability that a link from one node to another leads to the opposite link.

\begin{align} R = \dfrac{1}{2E} \text{tr}\left(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{A}^\mathrm{T}\right) \end{align}

An undirected network's reciprocity is trivially $1$ if each undirected edge is interpreted as a bidirected link. However, NetworkX's method reciprocity definitely returns zero if the input graph is undirected.

4. Assortativity¶

Assortativity measures how a node's identity affects its neighbourhood. An assortative network has homophilic nodes, which tend to connect to nodes similar to themselves; on the other hand, a dissortative network has heterophilic nodes, which tend to connect to nodes different from themselves. Since similarity between nodes can be defined with either categorical or quantitative attributes, assortativity is also divided into such two types.

4.1 Categorical assortativity¶

Categorical assortativity defines inter-node similiarity with nodes' enumerative attirbutes likes colours. If the $N$ nodes in a network are classified into $p$ classes, the network's assorativity coefficient amounts to

\begin{align} r_c = \dfrac{\sum_{s=1}^p {e_{ss} - a_sb_s}} {1-\sum_{s=1}^p {a_sb_s}}\,, \end{align}

where $e_{ss}$ is the fraction of edges that connects a node in class $s$ to another node in the same class; then, $a_s\equiv\sum_s{e_{st}}$ and $b_s\equiv\sum_t{e_{ts}}$ respectively measure the fraction of edges starting from and ending at nodes of class $s$. If the network is undirected, $e_{st}=e_{ts}$, so $a_s=b_s$ and $a_sb_s=a_s^2$.

While a perfectly assortative network, whose edges only connect nodes of the same class, has $r_c=1$, a neutral network has $r_c=0$ because $e_{ss}$ is expected to equal $a_sb_s$. Interestingly, a perfectly dissortative network, whose edges always connect nodes of different classes, has $r_c\in\left[-1,0\right)$ instead of $r_c=-1$, i.e. it may not hit $-1$ even if the network is perfectly dissortative. This intuitively means that a perfectly dissortative network is "closer" to a random network than a perfectly assortative network is. [Read Newman, M. E. J., Mixing patterns in networks. Phys. Rev. E, 67, 026126 (2003) for more discussion on this intuition.]

You can compute $r_c$ with attirbute_assortativity_coefficient.

In [20]:
nx.attribute_assortativity_coefficient(copperfield, 'value')
Out[20]:
-0.5060450250138964

The negative value means that the semantic network is dissortative. This makes sense because adjectives pair up with nouns more frequently than with adjectives, so do nouns with adjectives than with nouns.

Alternatively, you can get the matrix $\mathbf{e}=\left(e_{ij}\right)$ with attribute_mixing_matrix, which helps rewrite the definition of $r_c$ as:

\begin{align} r_c = \dfrac{\text{tr}{\,\mathbf{e}} - \lVert \mathbf{e}^2 \rVert} {1 - \lVert \mathbf{e}^2 \rVert}\,, \end{align}

where $\lVert\cdot\rVert$ sums up the elements of the matrix enclosed.

4.2 Modularity¶

The nominator of $r_c$ is commonly called the modularity $M$, which one may calculate with a different formulation. If a $k_i$-degree node $i$ belongs to class $c_i$,

\begin{align} M = \dfrac{1}{2E} \sum_{i,j} \left( A_{ij} - \dfrac{k_ik_j}{2E} \right) \delta \left(c_i, c_j\right)\,, \end{align}

where $\delta\left(x,y\right)=\begin{cases} 1 & \left(x=y\right) \\ 0 & \left(x\neq y\right)\end{cases}$ and we expect $\dfrac{k_ik_j}{2E}$ edges between nodes $i$ and $j$. (You will learn how to derive this expected value later.) It is equivalent to the original formulation because

\begin{align} \begin{cases} e_{st} &= &\dfrac{1}{2E} \sum_{i,j} A_{ij} \delta\left(c_i,s\right) \delta\left(c_j,t\right) \\ a_s &= &\dfrac{1}{2E} \sum_i k_i \delta \left(c_i,s\right)\, \end{cases}\,. \end{align}

Additionally, we may define a modularity matrix $\mathbf{B}=\mathbf{A}-\dfrac{1}{2E}kk^\mathrm{T}$, where $k=\left(\matrix{k_1\\\vdots\\k_N}\right)$, and a class matrix $\mathbf{\Delta}_{N\times p}=\left(\Delta_{is}\right)$ with $\Delta_{is}=\begin{cases}1 & (i\in s)\\0 & (i \not\in s)\end{cases}$. This helps reformulate $M$ as

\begin{align} M = \dfrac{1}{2E}\,\text{tr}{\,\left(\mathbf{\Delta}^T \mathbf{B} \, \mathbf{\Delta}\right)}\,. \end{align}

You can get the modularity matrix $\mathbf{B}$ with modularity_matrix.

In [22]:

D = np.array([[float(i in c) for c in [adjs, nouns]] for i in copperfield.nodes])

4.3 Quantitative assortativity¶

On the contrary to categorical assortativity, quantitative assortativity is associated with some quantitative attribute $Q$. One may measure quantitative assortativity with the Pearson correlation coefficient.

\begin{align} r_q &= \dfrac{\langle xy \rangle - \langle x \rangle \langle y \rangle} {\sqrt{\langle x^2 \rangle - \langle x \rangle^2} \sqrt{\langle y^2 \rangle - \langle y \rangle^2}}\, \\ &= \dfrac{\text{cov}{\left(x,y\right)}} {\text{sd}{\left(x\right)} \text{sd}{\left(y\right)}} \end{align}

The averages $\langle\cdot\rangle$ run through all edges, each of which connect a node with $Q=x$ to one with $Q=y$. If the network is undirected, $\text{sd}{\left(x\right)}=\text{sd}{\left(y\right)}$ reduces the denominator to $\text{var}{\left(x\right)}$.

You can compute $r_q$ with numeric_assortativity_coefficient. Particularly, you may use degree_assortativity_coefficient if the quantitive attribute that you are measuring is a node's degree.

In [23]:
for n,d in copperfield.nodes(data=True):
    d['degree'] = copperfield.degree[n]

 nx.degree_assortativity_coefficient(copperfield))

An alternatively definition of $r_q$ employs the modularity matrix $\mathbf{B}$ and the Laplacian matrix $\mathbf{L}$.

\begin{align} r_q = \dfrac{q^\mathrm{T}\,\mathbf{B}\,q} {q^\mathrm{T}\left(\mathbf{B}+\mathbf{L}\right)q} \,\,\text{with}\,\, q = \left(\matrix{q_1\\\vdots\\q_N}\right) \end{align}
In [24]:

k = np.array([[k] for n,k in nx.degree(copperfield)])
np.asscalar((k.T@B@k)/(k.T@(B+L)@k))

4.4 Degree correlation¶

If $Q$ measures a node's degree $k$, the correlation coefficient $r_q$ describes a network's degree correlation, whose sign helps us judge if the nodes assort accoding to degree. Alternatively, one may determine a network's degree assortativity with its correlation exponent $\mu$.

While node $i$'s average-neighbour-degree $k'_i=\langle{A_{ij}k_j}\rangle$, the expected averaged-neighbour-degree $\hat{k^\prime}(k)$ of a $k$-degree node reads

\begin{align} \hat{k^\prime}(k) = \dfrac{\sum_i k^\prime_i\,\delta\left(k_i, k\right)} {\sum_i \delta\left(k_i, k\right)}\,. \end{align}

Since connected nodes in an assortative network tend to have similar degrees, $\hat{k^\prime}$ increases with $k$; on the other hand, a dissortative network's $\hat{k^\prime}$ decreases as $k$ grows. The special case is a neutral network, on which $\hat{k^\prime}$ is a constant: $\hat{k^\prime}=\dfrac{\langle{k^2}\rangle}{\langle{k}\rangle}$. If we "assume" that $\hat{k^\prime}$ obeys a power law, i.e. $\hat{k^\prime} \sim k^\mu$ with a correlation exponent $\mu$, the sign of $\mu$ implies a network's assortativity as that of $r_q$ does. (You may check Barabási, A.-L., Network Science. Cambridge University Press, Cambridge (2016) for a more rigorous discussion.)

You can compute $k^\prime$ with average_degree_connectivity.
